    1. Tim Storms-10 Octaves

    Tim Storms of Missouri, USA, reportedly holds the record for both the broadest vocal range and the lowest note made by a human, as per the Guinness Book of World Records.

    In addition to having an astonishing 10 octaves of vocal range, Storms is also capable of producing a low G-7 at 0.189 Hz. The human ear is unable to hear this note since it is infrasonic and lower than seven octaves below a typical piano note.

    Along with setting world records, he has also collaborated with a variety of bands, including Acapella, Rescue, and ABV. From G/G#-5 to G/G#5, his vocal range is wide.

    FAQ

    What is a Vocal Range?

    The word “vocal range” broadly refers to the entire spectrum of notes that a singer’s voice is capable of producing, starting from the lowest note and extending to the highest note. Range, then, is the difference between a singer’s ability to sing at the highest and lowest notes, to put it another way.

    This extreme range of a person’s voice, which is made up of all unusable, utterable, but nondescript vocal noises and is measured from the lowest grunt to the highest vocal squeak that is possible, is also known as the “vocable compass.”

    The range of pitches a human voice can phonate is known as the vocal range. It is frequently used to categorize singing voices into voice types in the context of the song, where it serves as a defining quality.

    What is Octave

    An octave, often known as a perfect octave, in music is the distance between two pitches that have twice the frequency of one another. The usage of the octave relationship, which has been called the “fundamental miracle of music” and “common in most musical systems,” is a natural phenomenon.
